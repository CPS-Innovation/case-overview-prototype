# Show and tell 5: Helping lawyers review a case and share it with the relevant parties as early as possible

## The problem we're trying to solve

At the moment, case materials exist as a pile of documents, mostly PDF. 

There's nothing to connect a document to what it actually proves.

As a lawyer looks at the material, they're:

- deciding what the charge is and whether the material supports it
- spotting what needs redacting
- noticing what's missing

But the system doesn't capture that legal reasoning as the user is reviewing the case. 

As a result:

- Lawyers copy content out of CMS into personal tools to read and annotate it, then upload those notes back as more documents in the pile
- Review summaries and case notes are buried and hard to find; they may be low quality, out of date, and never read by the next person on the case
- When evidence changes — for example when a witness withdraws — there's no way to see the knock-on effect without re-reading the whole case from scratch
- The same analysis is repeated at multiple stages — initial review, IDPC, PTPH form, opening at trial. The lawyer ends up making the same judgement more than once
- Lawyers spend time on admin such as writing statements confirming they've complied with charging rules, rather than reviewing cases

And for users downstream:

- Reviews are either not done or are hard to understand later. It’s more difficult for the advocate, so they start early on the day of the hearing to understand the case — effectively doing the review from scratch, on top of everything else
- Defendants rarely receive the case before the first hearing, which doesn't comply with the rules. This matters because: if the prosecution case is clear to the defence early, they won't fight it, and the defendant will plead guilty sooner — fewer hearings, shorter hearings, fewer adjournments

All of this is long-winded, error prone and has a significant operational cost.

## Hypothesis 1

If lawyers can read, highlight and annotate case materials within the system — and those annotations connect directly to structured data like charges and evidence — then:

- Analysis is captured once and reused across the case journey, rather than repeated from scratch at every stage
- The lawyer or advocate can understand the case quickly from structured annotations, rather than hunting for a buried summary document or re-reading everything
- When evidence changes, the impact is immediately visible — showing which charges are affected without re-reading the case
- Lawyers will not have to leave the system to do their actual work which is more efficient

## Hypothesis 2

If completing a review notifies the right people and shares the right material automatically then:

- It will drastically reduce the administrative burden on users
- The quality of what gets shared is consistent and easier to understand
